Prime Numbers in Cryptography: Securing the Internet

Delve into how prime factorization forms the foundation of modern cryptography schemes like RSA, protecting your data online.

GH
Dr. Grace Hopper
Published July 7, 2026
12 min read 2,150 views
Hard

1. The Security Paradox

Every time you input your credit card number, log in to email, or load an HTTPS page, your browser relies on a paradox: it uses a public key that anyone can see to encrypt data, but requires a private key that only the recipient has to decrypt it. This asymmetry is at the heart of public-key cryptography.

The core mathematical engine behind this asymmetry is the prime number. Specifically, the fact that multiplying two large primes is easy, but factoring their product back into the original primes is incredibly difficult.

2. Prime Multiplication vs. Factorization

Consider two moderately sized prime numbers: \[ p = 61 \quad \text{and} \quad q = 53 \] Multiplying them is basic arithmetic: \[ n = p \times q = 61 \times 53 = 3233 \] Now imagine you are handed the number \( 3233 \) and asked to find its prime factors. Without prior knowledge, you would have to run test divisions by every prime up to \( \sqrt{3233} \approx 57 \).

In real-world security schemes (like RSA-2048), the prime factors chosen are hundreds of digits long. While a supercomputer can multiply them in milliseconds, finding the original factors from their product would take billions of years with modern computational resources. This is known as a one-way function with a trapdoor.

3. The RSA Encryption Scheme

Invented in 1977 by Ron Rivest, Adi Shamir, and Leonard Adleman, the RSA algorithm leverages the properties of prime numbers and modular arithmetic. Here is how key generation works:

  1. Choose two distinct large prime numbers \( p \) and \( q \).
  2. Compute their product \( n = pq \). The length of \( n \) is the key length.
  3. Compute Euler's totient function: \[ \phi(n) = (p-1)(q-1) \]
  4. Choose an integer \( e \) (the public exponent) such that \( 1 < e < \phi(n) \) and \( \gcd(e, \phi(n)) = 1 \). (Often, \( 65537 \) is used).
  5. Compute \( d \), the modular multiplicative inverse of \( e \pmod{\phi(n)} \), which satisfies: \[ de \equiv 1 \pmod{\phi(n)} \]
Keys Summary:
  • Public Key: \( (e, n) \) – distributed publicly to encrypt messages.
  • Private Key: \( (d, n) \) – kept secret to decrypt messages.

4. Modular Arithmetic Encrypting

To encrypt a message represented as an integer \( m \) (where \( 0 \le m < n \)): \[ c \equiv m^e \pmod n \] To decrypt the ciphertext \( c \): \[ m \equiv c^d \pmod n \] This restoration works because of Fermat's Little Theorem and Euler's Theorem, which mathematically link exponentiation modulo a product of primes back to the initial value.

5. Quantum Threats to Prime Cryptography

The security of RSA rests entirely on the assumption that prime factorization is slow. However, in 1994, Peter Shor published **Shor's Algorithm**, a quantum computer algorithm capable of finding prime factors in polynomial time.

When large-scale quantum computers become viable, traditional prime-based cryptography will be compromised, driving modern research toward **post-quantum cryptography** (such as lattice-based cryptography).
