Features of a Prime Number

Dive into the fundamental features, mathematical definitions, and structural properties that make prime numbers the atomic elements of mathematics.

AT
Prof. Alan Turing
Published July 1, 2026
5 min read 1,240 views
Easy

1. Definition of a Prime

A prime number is defined as a natural number greater than 1 that has exactly two positive divisors: 1 and itself. Natural numbers greater than 1 that are not prime are called composite numbers.

Definition: Let \( p \in \mathbb{N} \). We say \( p \) is prime if and only if: \[ p > 1 \quad \text{and} \quad \forall d \in \mathbb{N}, \; (d \mid p \implies d = 1 \lor d = p) \]

2. The Smallest Prime

The number 2 is the smallest prime number. Crucially, it is also the only even prime number. Every subsequent even number is divisible by 2, which automatically disqualifies them from being primes.

This special property gives 2 a unique role in arithmetic and number theory. It is the starting node for many mathematical induction proofs and algorithms.

3. Odd Primes

Aside from the number 2, all other prime numbers are odd. As a consequence, they can be written as either: \[ 4k + 1 \quad \text{or} \quad 4k + 3 \] for some integer \( k \). This classification divides the odd primes into two distinct sets, which play significant roles in quadratic reciprocity and sum-of-squares theorems.

4. Infinitude of Primes

There are infinitely many prime numbers. This was first proved by the ancient Greek mathematician Euclid in his work Elements (Book IX, Proposition 20).

Euclid's Theorem Proof Summary: Suppose there were only a finite list of primes \( p_1, p_2, \dots, p_n \). Consider the product of all these primes plus one: \[ N = (p_1 \times p_2 \times \dots \times p_n) + 1 \] Now, \( N \) is either prime or composite:

  • If \( N \) is prime, then we have found a prime not on our list (since \( N \) is larger than all of them).
  • If \( N \) is composite, it must have some prime factor \( q \). If \( q \) were one of the primes on our list, it would have to divide the difference \( N - (p_1 \times p_2 \times \dots \times p_n) = 1 \), which is impossible. Thus, \( q \) must be a prime not on our list.

In either case, our list of primes was incomplete, proving that prime numbers are indeed infinite.

5. The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic

Prime numbers are the absolute building blocks of the integers. The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic states that every integer \( n > 1 \) can be represented uniquely as a product of prime numbers, up to the order of the factors.

Theorem: Every integer \( n > 1 \) can be uniquely factored as: \[ n = p_1^{a_1} p_2^{a_2} \dots p_k^{a_k} \] where \( p_1 < p_2 < \dots < p_k \) are prime numbers, and each exponent \( a_i \ge 1 \).

This is why prime numbers are often described as the "atoms" of mathematics. By understanding the prime factorization of a number, we can deduce its divisors, totient counts, and other arithmetic properties.
